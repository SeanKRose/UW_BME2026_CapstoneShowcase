Project Description

PRISM is a pressure-sensing system for the interface between a transtibial prosthetic socket and the residual limb. It integrates a distributed array of sensors into a standard prosthetic sock. Calibrated to each user’s anatomy, PRISM measures localized pressure during daily use and presents the data as intuitive, real-time visual feedback. This helps users adjust sock plies throughout the day and enables clinicians to identify high-risk pressure regions, mitigate skin degradation, and make informed socket design decisions.

Overall Capstone Experience

Capstone Described in 3 Words:

Rewarding, Collaborative, Impactful

Favourite Capstone Memory

One of our favourite memories was visiting an actual prosthetic clinic (Prosthetic Ability) where we got to speak with experienced prosthetists to learn about their workflow and the challenges that they and their patients typically face. From this, we were able to better understand the magnitude and impact of the problem we were trying to solve. Of course, the BME symposium was another favourite memory of ours since we got to interact with so many people from different backgrounds, and discuss how our project could really impact the prosthetics industry, and of course - show off our prototype!

Biggest Challenges

As a team, we faced several debugging challenges during Capstone – things breaking at the least convenient time, design decisions we only retrospectively could realize were bad, etc. But, we learnt a lot as a team from them.

Takeaways

Our biggest takeaway from Capstone was talking to stakeholders - hearing firsthand about the real-life challenges amputees and clinicians face every day was eye-opening. Those conversations often reshaped how we solved the problem entirely. Learning about the current lack of solutions for pressure sores for amputees made the human connection to this work feel even more impactful!

Advice for Future BME Students

Choose a problem or topic that you are genuinely interested in and talk to as many experienced people as you can in that area. It’ll shape your trajectory a lot, and it's good to do early on! Also, apply for funding as early on as you can in the process (after 3B as soon as you have a solid project idea). That extra funding early on can help with initial R&D, and is nice for validating your problem space if you’re interested in entrepreneurship


What's Next?

PRISM has gained meaningful interest from clinicians – occupational therapists, doctors, and prosthetists – who recognize the problem and have seen our prototype firsthand, which is encouraging. Through our partnership with the Waterloo Microfluidics Lab, we can see the project continuing there or in a related space like the Neurorehabilitation Lab, whether by one of us or someone else entirely. Regardless of what comes next, our goal is to document everything we've learned and leave the project in a state where it's easy for anyone to pick up and run with
