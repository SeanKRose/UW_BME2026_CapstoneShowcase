Project Description

Many patients with potential heart problems are prescribed a continuous Holter monitor to record electrical heart activity in daily life. However, to obtain context for these signals, patients must keep physical journals to track symptoms and activity. The process is inconvenient, and often logs are left incomplete or inaccurate, limiting the clinical value of the data. We propose a discreet, multimodal Holter device that will reduce the need for manual journaling while automatically capturing contextual activity data, ensuring more reliable and interpretable information for physicians.

Biggest Challenges

  1. 1. When we began talking to stakeholders, we wanted to solve one problem - the burden of journalling. However, we quickly found that Holter monitors also have other issues such as skin irritation that a redesigned solution would not be complete without. Moving to address both journalling and irritation requirements through activity classification and dry electrode use, respectively, required extensive multi-disciplinary work to bring to life, as well as compromises between time and requirements.
  2. Dry electrode development required extensive validation and iteration to achieve comparable signal quality to sticky electrodes while also attempting to minimize movement artifacts. Each iteration presented various unexpected issues during validation, often leading us to more questions than answers and testing the depth of our debugging skills.

Takeaways

You don’t need to think that far outside to the box and design something fancy to solve a problem - relatively simple solutions can solve problems well.

Advice for Future BME Students

Start searching for problems to need to be addressed early in your degree. While searching for a problem, choose a very targeted problem (you can’t solve everything).
